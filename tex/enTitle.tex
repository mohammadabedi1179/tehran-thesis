\gdef\@en-abstract{%
Autonomous navigation of ground vehicles in dynamic environments requires the simultaneous integration of environmental perception, motion decision-making, obstacle avoidance, safety supervision, and accurate execution of control commands. This problem is particularly challenging for a four-wheel-drive scooter because of its relatively large mass and inertia, limited stopping capability, unequal motor responses, sensor uncertainty, and the presence of moving obstacles. This thesis develops and experimentally evaluates a learning-based framework for goal-directed navigation of a four-wheel-drive scooter among static and dynamic obstacles.

The proposed policy is based on a recurrent Soft Actor--Critic architecture, referred to as LSTM--SAC, which generates continuous linear and angular velocity commands. A teacher--student training scheme is employed to reduce the gap between ideal simulation observations and sensor-based implementation. The teacher agent is trained using accurate geometric information, whereas the student agent learns from observations that more closely represent the actual sensing pipeline. Curriculum learning is used to increase environmental complexity progressively. In addition, a safety shield evaluates obstacle distance, relative motion, and estimated time to collision before the commands are applied.

The complete practical system integrates a stereo camera, target and obstacle detection and tracking, depth estimation, an inertial measurement unit, wheel encoders, ultrasonic sensors, and closed-loop control of four drive motors. In the most challenging simulated scenario, the sensor-based student agent with the active safety shield achieved a success rate of 96\% with no collisions. In real-world evaluation over 20 independent episodes, the integrated system achieved an 80\% success rate and a 5\% collision rate.

The results demonstrate that combining deep reinforcement learning, teacher--student training, multi-sensor perception, and an independent safety layer can enable practical goal-directed navigation of the scooter. Nevertheless, perception errors during motion, motor-control limitations, dependence on the safety shield, and the remaining simulation-to-reality gap continue to limit fully reliable autonomous operation.%
